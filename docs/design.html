The Shift from Netdecking to Sealed-First

The netdeck movement promised consistency and speed. But 2026 has revealed the true cost of copying lists without understanding them: stale metas, brittle play patterns, and games that feel solved before they begin.

The Origins II limited environment (which released yesterday) confirmed what judges have been signaling for months: sealed-first formats reward genuine card evaluation over memorized answers.

Three foundational principles
  1. Card evaluation from the ground up. Every pick gets judged in context, not against a tier list.
  2. Synergy as a design requirement. The pool you open enforces creative deckbuilding naturally.
  3. Flexible strategy without meta gaps. Whatever you open, your fundamentals carry the game.

Do's and Don'ts

Do

  • Set every heading in Newsreader and every functional string in Geist.
  • Build depth from surface steps and hairline borders; shadows for modals, drawers, menus, and toasts only.
  • Keep the paper-and-lacquer warmth: warm neutrals, brown-tinted shadows, earthy semantics.
  • Use label (10px, 0.04em, uppercase) for all micro-text: table headers, badges, eyebrows.
  • Give card grids catalog-like breathing room: 24px padding, 16px+ gutters.
  • Show money in tabular numerals; color gains moss and losses crimson, and pair every price with a freshness chip.

Don't

  • No blue-purple gradients, glassmorphism, neon glows, or generic-SaaS styling.
  • No cold pure grays or stark #FFFFFF; every neutral carries the warm cast.
  • No heavy shadow stacks or saturated accents; the system is quiet and flat.
  • No kitsch-Japanese ornament: no cherry blossoms, torii gates, brush-script fonts.
  • Never headings in Geist, body in Newsreader, or a third typeface.
  • Don't over-round: cards cap at md (12px), buttons at sm (8px); pills only for chips, secondary buttons, and segmented controls.
